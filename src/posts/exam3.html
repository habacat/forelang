Part III Reading Comprehension（40 分钟）

Section A（Questions 13–20）

Passage 1 – Remote Work and Small Towns

For many years, young professionals who wanted a successful career felt that they had no choice but to move to big cities. Most well-paid jobs, important meetings and networking events seemed to be concentrated in tall office buildings in a few major business centers. Long commutes and high rents were treated as an unavoidable price to pay for opportunity.

However, the rapid development of remote work has begun to change this picture. With a stable Internet connection and a laptop, an employee can attend online meetings from almost anywhere. Some workers have chosen to leave expensive city centers and move to smaller towns or suburbs, where they can enjoy lower housing costs and a slower pace of life while still keeping their original jobs.

Yet remote work is far from a perfect solution. When your office is also your bedroom or kitchen table, the boundary between work and life can easily become blurred. Many remote workers find themselves checking e-mails late at night, or feeling guilty if they are not always “online”. The very technology that gives them more freedom can also make them feel that they should be available at all times.

The rise of remote workers has also brought both opportunities and challenges to small towns. On the one hand, newcomers bring fresh spending power to local cafés, bookstores and co-working spaces. They may start new businesses, organize cultural events and introduce different ideas. On the other hand, if too many well-paid workers arrive in a short time, housing prices may quickly go up. Local families can be pushed out of neighborhoods where they have lived for generations, a process often described as “gentrification”.

In response, some local governments are trying to attract remote workers in a more balanced way. They improve digital infrastructure, build shared offices and encourage newcomers to take part in community projects. At the same time, they discuss how to protect long-term residents from sharp rent increases. Whether remote work will permanently reshape the map of where people live is still unclear, but it has already forced both cities and small towns to rethink what a “good place to live” really means.

Section B（Questions 21–28）

Passage 2 – Digital Detox Tourism

On many travel websites today, it is easy to find advertisements for “digital detox camps”. These holidays promise to take people to peaceful mountains, quiet villages or remote islands where mobile phones must be locked away and Wi-Fi is turned off. For a few days, participants are encouraged to forget about social media, e-mail and online meetings, and to enjoy long walks, face-to-face conversations and simple daily routines.

The popularity of such tours shows how tired many people have become of constant notifications and endless scrolling. Office workers complain that they can never fully relax because messages from colleagues or clients may arrive at any moment. Parents worry that their children spend more time with screens than with friends. Some people find it hard to focus on a single book or task for more than a few minutes. For them, going on a digital detox holiday seems like a chance to press a “reset” button.

However, critics point out that digital detox tourism is not a perfect answer. First of all, many of these tours are expensive. Only people with enough money and free time can afford to fly to an island just to switch off their phones. In addition, even if tourists are offline, the staff who cook their meals or drive their buses may still rely on smartphones to do their jobs. As a result, some argue that digital detox has become a kind of “luxury problem” that mainly belongs to people who already have many choices in life.

The idea of completely escaping technology for a few days may also send the wrong message. If people treat the trip as a magic cure, they may simply return to exactly the same online habits once they get home. Many experts suggest that what really matters is not a short holiday, but everyday decisions: putting the phone in another room at night, turning off unnecessary notifications, or setting clear times to check messages. In this sense, a digital detox tour might be a helpful starting point, but it only works when travellers are willing to change how they use technology in their daily lives.
