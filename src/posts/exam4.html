Passage 1: Why People Collect Things (Questions 15–19)

Walk into almost any home and you are likely to find a collection of some kind: stamps, coins, action figures, coffee mugs, postcards, or even houseplants. To outsiders, these objects may look like ordinary items that simply take up space. To collectors, however, they form a personal museum that tells a story about who they are and what they value.

Psychologists say that people collect things for several reasons. One reason is the search for identity. A person who loves travel may collect train tickets or hotel key cards to remind themselves that they are curious and adventurous. Collections also give people a sense of control. In a world that often feels chaotic, arranging and caring for objects in a particular order can make life feel more predictable and manageable.

Collecting can also be social. People who share a similar interest — for example, fans of a certain sports team — often trade items and meet at fairs or online forums. Through these activities, they build friendships and a feeling of belonging. In some cases, collections gain financial value. Rare coins or first-edition books may become a kind of informal investment, though serious collectors usually say that emotional value matters more than money.

Of course, collecting can go too far. When people are unable to throw anything away and their homes become dangerously crowded, professionals may describe the situation as hoarding rather than collecting. The difference lies in purpose and enjoyment. A healthy collector knows roughly what they are looking for and can still live comfortably without every single item. A hoarder, by contrast, feels deep anxiety at the thought of letting go.

Passage 2: The Debate over Daylight Saving Time (Questions 20–24)

Twice a year, many people move their clocks forward or backward by one hour. This system, known as daylight saving time (DST), was first widely introduced during the early twentieth century. At that time, supporters believed that longer evening daylight would save energy and help factories and shops operate more efficiently.

Today, however, the benefits of DST are under debate. Modern research suggests that the system may not save much electricity, because people now use energy for many different purposes, such as air conditioning and electronic devices. In some regions, longer evenings in summer even mean that people drive more, which can increase fuel use and pollution.

Health experts also raise questions about the practice. When clocks move forward in spring, people suddenly lose one hour of sleep. Studies have found a small but noticeable rise in traffic accidents and heart problems in the days after this change. For people who already have sleep difficulties, the sudden shift can be especially challenging.

Despite these concerns, many governments have been slow to change the system. Some businesses like the fact that people are more willing to shop or eat out when it stays light later in the evening. Tourism officials also argue that longer daylight hours help local attractions. Yet an increasing number of citizens and scientists are calling for a simpler solution: choose one time and keep it all year.

Passage 3: Can Plants Learn? (Questions 25–29)

For a long time, scientists avoided using words like "memory" or "learning" to describe plants. These terms seemed too closely connected with animals and human brains. Plants do not have nervous systems, so it felt risky to suggest that they could behave in similar ways. In recent years, however, a series of experiments has reopened the question.

In one famous study, researchers worked with a climbing plant that normally grows toward light. They placed a small fan next to the plant and turned the fan on shortly before switching on a light from the opposite direction. After repeating this pairing many times, the plant began to bend toward the fan even when no light followed. To the researchers, this suggested that the plant had learned to treat the sound and air from the fan as a signal for light.

Not all scientists agree with this interpretation. Some argue that the plant might simply have been reacting to air movement or other physical changes in a mechanical way. They caution against giving plants human-like qualities, because such language can be misleading. Nevertheless, more and more studies are finding that plants respond differently depending on their past experiences, such as previous exposure to drought, salt, or insect attack.

Whether this should be called learning is still debated. What most researchers do accept is that plants are far more active than they appear. They exchange chemical messages through their roots and the air, adjust their growth to compete for light, and in some cases even warn nearby plants of danger. The next time you walk through a forest, you may feel that the plants around you are quietly paying attention.

Section B (Questions 30–34)

Passage 4: Preparing for an Uncertain Job Market

For many years, a common picture of success was simple: study hard, graduate, and then find a stable job with one company. In return for loyalty and good performance, workers expected a steady salary and benefits until retirement. In today's economy, this picture is rapidly disappearing. Technology, automation and global competition are changing jobs so quickly that many experts say the idea of a "job for life" is becoming outdated.

Universities face growing pressure to prepare students for this uncertain job market. Some people argue that schools should train students for very specific occupations, such as data analyst or financial engineer. Others believe that this approach is too narrow and may leave graduates unprepared when these jobs change or disappear. Instead, they suggest that universities should focus on teaching transferable skills — abilities that can be used in many different fields.

A recent international survey asked employers what they value most when hiring new graduates. Surprisingly, many said that a student's major was less important than skills such as critical thinking, communication and the ability to learn quickly. Employers also mentioned curiosity, teamwork and a willingness to solve unfamiliar problems. These qualities are hard to measure on paper, so companies increasingly look at what applicants have actually done.

For students, this means that experience matters. Internships, part-time jobs, volunteer work and research projects all help them test their interests and show evidence of their abilities. Instead of waiting passively for the "perfect" job after graduation, students are encouraged to build a portfolio of projects that demonstrate real skills: a website they designed, a marketing plan they developed, or an experiment they helped run in a laboratory.

The article concludes that no single program can guarantee success in such a fast-changing world. However, students can improve their chances by combining solid academic knowledge with practical experience and by developing a habit of lifelong learning. In a sense, graduation should not be seen as the end of education, but as the beginning of a career-long process of updating one's skills and understanding.
